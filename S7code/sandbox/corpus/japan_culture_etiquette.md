# Japanese Culture and Visitor Etiquette

Japan's culture emphasizes harmony (wa), respect (sonkei), and attention to detail. Understanding key customs will enrich your experience and avoid inadvertent offenses.

## Greetings and Body Language

**Bowing** is the Japanese greeting. The depth conveys respect:
- Casual 15° bow: acquaintances, shop staff
- 30° bow: clients, supervisors, formal meetings
- 45° bow: deep apology or gratitude

Handshakes occur in business contexts with foreign visitors. Never hug or kiss on the cheek — personal space is valued.

**Business cards (meishi)**: Present and receive with both hands. Study a card before setting it down respectfully. Never write on a card or put it in your pocket immediately.

## Shoes and Indoor Spaces

Always remove shoes when entering a home, traditional ryokan (inn), and some restaurants with tatami seating. Look for a genkan (entrance area) below floor level — this signals shoes-off. Wear clean socks.

Separate toilet slippers are often provided in bathrooms — change into them when entering and crucially change back when leaving.

## Eating and Drinking

- **Itadakimasu** before eating (a humble "I receive this meal")
- **Gochisosama deshita** after eating (thanks to those who prepared the meal)
- Do not stick chopsticks vertically into rice — it resembles incense sticks at funerals
- Do not pass food chopstick-to-chopstick — this mimics a funeral cremation ritual
- Slurping noodles is acceptable and complimentary
- It is considered rude to eat while walking in most contexts
- Drinking on trains is generally fine; eating on local trains (not shinkansen) is frowned upon
- Tipping is not practiced in Japan — it can cause offense

## Public Behavior

- Speak quietly on public transportation; phone calls are discouraged
- Queuing is sacred — always form orderly lines
- Move to the left on escalators (right in Osaka), keeping the right side free for those in a hurry
- Tattoos may prevent entry to onsen (hot spring baths) and some public pools
- Public displays of affection beyond hand-holding are considered inappropriate

## Temples and Shrines

At Shinto shrines:
1. Bow at the torii gate before entering
2. Wash hands at the temizuya (purification fountain)
3. Two bows, two claps, one bow before the main hall
4. Ema (prayer plaques) can be purchased and hung

At Buddhist temples: offer coins in the offertory box, ring the bell if present, pray quietly with palms together.

Photography is usually permitted outdoors; check signage inside buildings.

## Gift-Giving

Gift-giving (omiyage) is an important social ritual:
- Bring regional specialty foods (omiyage) when returning from trips
- Wrap gifts carefully — presentation matters as much as content
- Gifts are traditionally not opened in front of the giver
- Odd numbers (especially 4 and 9) are avoided — 4 sounds like "death" (shi), 9 like "suffering" (ku)

## Language Tips

Learning a few Japanese phrases shows respect:
- **Sumimasen** (すみません): Excuse me / Sorry
- **Arigatou gozaimasu** (ありがとうございます): Thank you (formal)
- **Eigo ga hanasemasu ka?** (英語が話せますか？): Do you speak English?
- **~wa doko desu ka?** (~はどこですか？): Where is ~?

Many signs in Tokyo are in English, and English menus are common in tourist areas.
